EU electricity market · a proposed metric

The Price of Coordination

Coupling bidding zones is supposed to lower the total cost of electricity. But coordinating markets faster than you build the grid has its own bill — redispatch, interconnector capital, and transfers between zones. This metric nets that bill against what coupling actually saves, and asks the blunt question: in a given year, would going local have been cheaper?

PoC(t) = [ Redispatch + Interconnector capex + Loop-flows + Coordination overhead + w·Distributional transfer ]  −  [ Dispatch savings + Reserve pooling + Avoided curtailment + Security of supply ]
PoC > 0 → coordinating cost more than going local  ·  PoC < 0 → coordination paid for itself

How to read this. Everything above the black line is a cost of running a coupled system; everything below is a saving it delivers. The bold line is the net. Where it crosses zero is the year coordination stops paying for itself under your assumptions.

Illustrative defaults, grounded in: ACER put EU congestion-management cost at ≈ €4 bn in 2023 (~60% in Germany); the Commission’s JRC projects remedial-action costs of ≈ €11–26 bn/yr by 2030 and €34–103 bn/yr by 2040 if the grid expands only at historic rates. On the benefit side, ENTSO-E’s “zero-trade” counterfactual valued market coupling at ≈ €34 bn in 2021, while its TYNDP 2024 marginal case links ≈ €6 bn/yr of new interconnection to ≈ €13 bn/yr of added welfare. Numbers here are stylised for exploration — swap in your own series from the ENTSO-E Transparency Platform and ACER Market Monitoring Reports before citing anything.
